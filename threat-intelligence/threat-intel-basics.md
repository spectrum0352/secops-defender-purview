Like gathering intelligence in warfare, CTI involves collecting and analysing data on cyber threats. This helps organizations:
•	Proactively build defenses: By understanding attacker motives, targets, and tactics (TTPs), organizations can anticipate potential attacks and implement effective security measures.
Data Sources for CTI:
•	Open-source intelligence (OSINT): Publicly available information like news articles, social media posts, and blogs.
•	Closed-source intelligence (CSINT): Government reports and intelligence briefings, not publicly available.
•	Threat sharing communities: Groups of organizations sharing cyber threat information.
•	Security vendors: Collect and analyze threat data from their customers.
•	Human intelligence (HUMINT): Information gathered from human sources in the field.
•	Geospatial intelligence (GEOINT): Intelligence derived from satellite and aerial imagery.
•	Signals intelligence (SIGINT): Intelligence obtained by intercepting electronic signals.
Using CTI for Security:
•	Threat hunting: Helps identify known threats and develop new detection methods.
•	Incident response: Provides information about attackers, tactics, and goals to aid in effective response.
•	Risk assessment: Helps organizations evaluate their cyber threat vulnerability.
•	Security planning: Informs development and implementation of security controls to mitigate threats.
Real-world Examples of CTI:
•	A financial services company uses CTI to identify and stop a phishing campaign targeting its employees.
•	A healthcare organization uses CTI to identify a ransomware attack, understand its details, and take steps to recover.
•	A government agency uses CTI to assess the risk of being targeted by a specific nation-state actor.
•	A retail company uses CTI to identify threats targeting them during the holiday season and develop appropriate security measures.
Overall, CTI empowers organizations to:
•	Gain valuable insights into the latest cyber threats.
•	Proactively defend against cyberattacks.
•	Improve their overall security posture.

In essence, cyber threat intelligence (CTI) is all about gathering and analyzing information on cyber threats in real-time. This helps organizations understand the threat landscape, identify potential attacks, and proactively defend their systems.
Here's a breakdown of the key points:
•	What it is: CTI is a cyclical process that involves planning, collecting data on threats, analyzing it, and sharing the insights to defend against cyberattacks.
•	Benefits: CTI helps organizations in several ways:
o	Monitor known threat actors who might target them.
o	Identify potential attacks using Indicators of Compromise (IOCs) shared by others.
o	Detect threats that could damage their brand reputation.
o	Uncover internal security risks.
•	How it works: CTI platforms collect data from various sources, analyze it to understand attacker motivations, targets, and tactics (TTPs). This intelligence allows organizations to build better defenses and stay ahead of potential attacks.
Think of CTI as gathering intel about your enemy in a cyber war. By understanding their strategies and goals, you can prepare your defenses and counter their attacks more effectively.

Cyber Threat Intelligence is a data that is collected, processed, and analysed to understand the threat actor’s motives, targets and attack behaviours. 

CTI is collected from a variety of sources, including:
•	Open-source intelligence (OSINT) is publicly available information, such as social media posts, news articles, and blog posts.
•	Closed-source intelligence (CSINT) is information that is not publicly available, such as government reports and intelligence briefings.
•	Threat sharing communities are groups of organizations that share information about cyber threats.
•	Security vendors collect and analyse threat data from their customers.
•	HUMINT (Human) is an intelligence type obtained from a person in the field.
•	GEOINT (Geographical) is an intelligence type obtained using satellite and aerial photographs.
•	SIGINT (Signal) is a type of intelligence obtained by interfering with signals.

CTI can be used for
•	Threat hunting: Threat hunting is the process of searching for and identifying malicious activity on networks and systems. CTI can be used to help organizations identify known threats and to develop new threat detection rules.
•	Incident response: CTI can be used to help organizations respond to cyberattacks by providing information about the threat actor, their tactics and techniques, and their potential goals.
•	Risk assessment: CTI can be used to help organizations assess their risk of being impacted by cyber threats.
•	Security planning: CTI can be used to help organizations develop and implement security controls to mitigate cyber threats.

CTI is an important data for organizations of all sizes to protect themselves from cyber threats. By using CTI, organizations can gain insights into the latest threats and take steps to protect themselves.

Here are some examples of how cyber threat intelligence can be used in practice:
•	A security analyst at a financial services company uses CTI to identify a new phishing campaign targeting employees of financial services companies. The analyst can develop a new rule to detect the phishing emails and prevent employees from falling victim to the attack.
•	A security incident response team at a healthcare organization uses CTI to identify a ransomware attack that has been launched against the organization. The team can use the CTI to identify the ransomware strain, the vulnerabilities that were exploited, and the attacker's IP addresses. The team is then able to take steps to mitigate the attack and recover from it.
•	A risk management team at a government agency uses CTI to assess the risk of being targeted by a nation-state actor. The team is able to identify the nation-state actor's capabilities, their target industries, and their latest tactics and techniques. The team then uses this information to develop risk mitigation strategies.
•	A security planning team at a retail company uses CTI to develop a security plan for the upcoming holiday season. The team is able to identify the known threats that are targeting retail companies during the holiday season. The team then uses this information to develop security controls to mitigate the threats.

CTI is a powerful tool that can help organizations detect, prevent, and respond to cyber threats. By using CTI, organizations can improve their overall security posture and reduce their risk of being impacted by cyberattacks.

Digital Threats
Non-physical threats target information systems and data. Here are some common types:
•	Malware: Malicious software like Trojans, worms, and viruses that can steal data, corrupt systems, or disrupt operations.
•	Adware: Unwanted software that bombards users with ads.
•	Spyware: Software that gathers information about users and their activities without their knowledge.
•	Denial-of-Service (DoS) and Distributed DoS (DDoS) Attacks: Attempts to overwhelm a system with traffic, making it unavailable to legitimate users.
•	Phishing: Deceptive emails or messages designed to trick users into revealing personal information or clicking malicious links.
•	Unauthorized Access: Gaining access to a system or data without permission.
•	Keyloggers: Software that records keystrokes, potentially capturing passwords and other sensitive information.

Types of Threats

This summary categorizes the different threats organizations face:
Internal Threats:
•	Insider Threats: These are the most concerning as they involve people with authorized access. They can be:
o	Disgruntled Insiders: Employees with a grudge who intentionally harm the system.
o	Accidental Insiders: Employees who make mistakes that lead to breaches (clicking phishing links).
o	External Insiders: Unauthorized individuals who gain access through someone inside.
•	Weak Security Controls: Poor passwords or inadequate access controls make it easier for attackers to get in.
•	System Vulnerabilities: Weaknesses in software or hardware that attackers can exploit.
External Threats:
•	Cybercriminals: They use various methods (malware, phishing, DoS) for financial gain, espionage, or just a challenge.
•	Competitors: They resort to unethical means (stealing trade secrets, website attacks) to gain an advantage.
•	Hacktivists: These are individuals or groups using technology for a political or social cause, sometimes attacking organizations they believe are wrongdoers.
•	Nation-States: They may attack for espionage or to disrupt critical infrastructure.
Additional Threats:
•	Physical Threats: Theft, vandalism, fire, and natural disasters can damage physical assets and disrupt operations.
•	Human Threats: These are unintentional errors by employees (e.g., deleting files) or a lack of security awareness.
•	Organizational Threats: Financial problems, regulatory compliance, and reputational damage can negatively impact the organization.
•	Natural and Environmental Threats: Climate change, pollution, and natural disasters can disrupt operations and cause financial losses.
By understanding these threats, you can take steps to develop a comprehensive security strategy to protect your organization.

Internal threats vs. External threats
•	Familiarity: People become accustomed to seeing the same person around, lowering suspicion.
•	Extensive access: Insiders have legitimate access to information and systems, bypassing external security measures.
•	Trusted Access: Insiders already have legitimate access to systems and data, bypassing firewalls and other security measures designed to keep external threats out. This allows them to move within the network undetected.
•	Knowledge of Weaknesses: Employees often have intimate knowledge of an organization's security protocols, vulnerabilities, and valuable data. They can exploit these weaknesses to bypass security measures or target specific information.
•	Reduced Suspicion: Because they are trusted insiders, their actions might not raise red flags. Daily activities can be disguised as legitimate work, making it harder to detect malicious intent.

The analogy of someone poking around unnoticed highlights this point. An external attacker would likely trigger alarms trying to gain access, while an insider can move around freely.

Cyber Threat Areas
Cyber threat activities can be categorized into three primary areas:

Tactical Level
•	Focus: Immediate threat detection and response.
•	Activities: Malware analysis, threat indicator ingestion, incident response.
•	Key roles: SOC analysts, SIEM teams, network security teams.

Operational Level
•	Focus: Understanding adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs).
•	Activities: Threat hunting, vulnerability management, incident response planning.
•	Key roles: Threat hunters, SOC analysts, incident response teams.

Strategic Level
•	Focus: Long-term security strategy and decision-making.
•	Activities: Threat intelligence analysis, risk assessment, security policy development.
•	Key roles: CISO, CIO, CTO, executive leadership.
By operating across these three levels, organizations can build a comprehensive cybersecurity posture.
